Status and Conservation Risk of Threatened Bull Trout

Columbia Headwaters and Saint Mary Recovery Units

Project Background

Bull trout (Salvelinus confluentus) are a cold-water obligate salmonid listed as Threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. Across the Columbia Headwaters and Saint Mary Recovery Units, the only populations east of the Continental Divide, many local populations have declined in recent decades due to a combination of habitat degradation, warming and altered stream hydrology, and the spread of non-native fishes.

Despite decades of monitoring effort, our ability to rigorously assess population status and conservation risk has been limited by a lack of data-driven, quantitative approaches operating at scales relevant to management. Single-population trend analyses often have low statistical power, can be sensitive to the time window of available data, and fail to capture the hierarchical structure of bull trout populations, from local spawning streams, to core area metapopulations, to the recovery unit as a whole.

This project was conceived to address that gap. In 2021, a team from the USGS Northern Rocky Mountain Science Center (NOROCK) and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service was awarded funding through the USGS-FWS Science Support Partnership to develop a unified, spatiotemporal status andthreat assessment for bull trout in these two recovery units. This website presents the products of that effort.

What We Did

The project was organized around three objectives, each corresponding to a section of this website.

1 — Centralized Database

We assembled and synthesized redd count monitoring data from 80 local bull trout populations across 26 core areas, spanning 1984–2022. Environmental covariates (stream temperature, summer base-flow, invasive fish proportion, and lake trout abundance) were compiled from state, federal, and tribal partners and linked to each population at annual resolution.

→ Explore the data

2 — Hierarchical Multi-Population Viability Analysis

We developed a hierarchical state-space Ricker model that integrates all available abundance data across local populations and core areas simultaneously. This approach borrows strength across populations to estimate demographic rates for data-sparse populations, propagates observation and process uncertainty through the analysis, and directly estimates the effects of abiotic and biotic threats on population growth rates (λ) at the local population and core area scales.

→ View model results

3 — Decision-Support Tools

We translated model outputs into an annually-updatable conservation prioritization framework designed to help resource managers identify which populations are most at risk and which management levers (invasive species control, flow augmentation, temperature reduction) offer the greatest potential benefit per population and per core area.

Species Background

Bull trout require cold (< 13 °C), clean, complex, and connected streams for all life stages. They are highly sensitive to a suite of anthropogenic stressors:

Threat Mechanism
Warming water temperatures Upper spawning threshold ~13 °C; lethal above ~20 °C; accelerated warming under climate change
Altered hydrology Low summer base-flow reduces habitat availability; high winter floods scour incubating eggs
Non-native fish Competitive displacement by brook trout and brown trout; predation and competition from lake trout in lake-connected systems
Migratory barriers Loss of access to adfluvial (lake-resident) life history reduces productivity; adfluvial populations support higher λ
Habitat degradation Road-related fine sediment impairs gravel spawning habitat; riparian degradation increases thermal loading

Bull trout population structure is hierarchical: local spawning populations are nested within core areas (roughly watershed-scale metapopulations), which in turn comprise recovery units. Effective conservation planning must account for dynamics at all three scales. This is a central motivation for the multi-population modeling approach used here.
