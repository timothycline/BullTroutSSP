Bull Trout Status and Threats

Multi-Population Viability Analysis

Authors: Timothy Cline, Montana State University (corresponding author timothy.cline2@montana.edu) Clint Muhlfeld, USGS - Northern Rocky Mountain Science Center Robert Al-Chokhachy, USGS - Northern Rocky Mountain Science Center

Bull trout (Salvelinus confluentus) are a cold-water obligate salmonid native to western North America and listed as Threatened under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. This site presents a hierarchical state-space population model developed through a collaboration between the US Geological Survey, US Fish and Wildlife Service, Montana Fish Wildlife and Parks, and Montana State University. The goal is to synthesize redd count monitoring data across the Northern Rocky Mountains and assess population status, threats, and vulnerability to inform conservation strategies.

What this model does

The model integrates long-term redd count time series (1984–2022) from 80 local populations nested within 26 core areas to estimate:

  • Population-specific growth rates and how they vary with environmental conditions
  • Density dependence at the population level
  • Effects of climate (summer streamflow, August water temperature) on population growth
  • Biotic threats from non-native trout and lake trout
  • Habitat quality indicators (adfluvial life history access, valley bottom extent, W95 winter flood frequency, road density)

About the model

The model is a hierarchical state-space Ricker model fitted with TMB (Template Model Builder). Process error follows an AR(1) structure; redd counts are modelled with a Negative Binomial (NB2) observation model to account for overdispersion. Where available, electrofishing CPUE data provides an independent abundance index via a catchability sub-model. Random effects are integrated out via the Laplace approximation. Parameter uncertainty is propagated via the delta method.

Species background

Bull trout require cold, clear, complex streams for spawning and rearing. They are possibly sensitive to:

Threat Mechanism
Warming water temperatures Upper thermal tolerance ~13–15 °C for spawning adults; lethal above ~20 °C
Altered hydrology Peak flow timing and winter flood frequency affect gravel incubation success
Non-native trout Competitive displacement and hybridization (brown trout, brook trout); predation (lake trout)
Road density Fine sediment delivery to spawning gravels; barrier culverts
Lack or loss of migratory access Adfluvial populations (lake-resident adults) have higher productivity
